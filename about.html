Why a new voting system?

Political shit flinging is nothing new, but it has been varying in ways that are cause for great concern.

  • A survey published by Pew Research in 2014 reveals that Americans have become more and more polarized, holding more and more consistently liberal or conservative opinions and having more and more negative opinions of the opposite side. In other words, nuance in people’s political opinions tends to vanish. In fact, many American presidents from a few decades ago would have looked like centrists by today’s standards.
  • An article published on Politico in October 2020 raises concern for the increasing share of both Democrat and Republican voters who think the use of political violence is justified.
Although there are many ways to explain these trends, one particularly convinced me: our electoral systems are fundamentally flawed in ways that incentivize these kinds of behaviors.

It is difficult to be comprehensive about the many ways in which the different electoral systems used around the world are flawed, but some patterns are easy to notice:

  • the United States electoral system only makes two parties viable in the long run, which reinforces a political duopoly between two parties that have little incentive to actually serve the people who elect them;
  • despite being more allowing of small parties, the French electoral system still doesn’t eliminate the high barrier between large and small parties, and strategical voting is still a matter of discussion almost every electoral cycle.
There is no reason whatsoever for political parties to be elected for their size rather than their ideas, and electors having to lie on their ballot to maximize their chances sounds like it came from a dystopian TV show. In the long run, it is not crazy at all to suspect these flaws are at least partially behind the huge social problems described above.

A theory of electoral systems?

As strange as it sounds, there does exist a theory of electoral systems, and in order to make it clear what a good electoral system is, it is important to talk about it.

An election is a game in the sense of game theory. It involves a finite number of players, called electors, who each make a decision by casting their ballot, and the outcome of the game depends on the decision of all players. To each possible outcome of the game is assigned a utility value that is subjective to each player and quantifies how satisfied that player is with that outcome.

More specifically, an election is a simultaneous game. This means that electors make their decision not knowing the other electors’ decisions. Of course, electors may disclose whom they voted for, but:

  • they may lie;
  • it is not intrinsic to the act of voting.

In an electoral system, all electors are provided with a finite set of alternatives, which is a more general term for candidate. Through their ballots, they then specify their preferences in a format that the electoral system allows. In a uninominal electoral system, the ballot only contains one alternative. In an ordinal electoral system, the ballot specifies a total order between the alternatives.

In a game, players have to make decisions based on the information that they have. Information may be:

  • public: available to everyone;
  • private: known only to one player who has the discretion to disclose it or not (examples include what the player believes or wants).
A strategy is an algorithm that takes all the information a player has access to and outputs a decision. A deterministic strategy will always output the same decision given the same information. In particular, it doesn’t involve randomness.

Many technical terms describe qualities we might expect from electoral systems. The most interesting ones are described in the table below.

QualityMeaning
Unanimity If all electors specify that A is their favorite alternative, then A is necessarily elected.
Majority If more than half of the electors specify that A is their favorite alternative, then A is necessarily elected.
Condorcet criterion If there is a unique alternative A that is pairwise favored over all other alternatives by over half of the electors, then A is necessarily elected.
Independence of irrelevant alternatives The probability that the electoral system favors A over B cannot change by adding or removing a third, unrelated alternative.
Strategyproofness In order to maximize their expected utility, the best strategy electors have is to make sure their ballot truly reflects their preferences. In other words, lying on the ballot is always a sub-optimal strategy.
Dictatorship There is a particular elector, called dictator, whose ballot completely determines the outcome of the election. The dictator may not be statically defined.
Lotocracy A form of dictatorship where the dictator is chosen randomly. It essentially consists of picking one ballot randomly and choosing the outcome from it alone.

Some immediate theorems:

  • Majority implies unanimity.
  • Condorcet criterion implies majority.
  • Dictatorship implies unanimity.
  • Lotocracy implies dictatorship.
  • Lotocracy implies independence of irrelevant alternatives.
  • Lotocracy implies strategyproofness.
It may seem surprising that lotocracy achieves both strategyproofness and independence of irrelevant alternatives so easily, but the reason is quite simple: relying entirely on luck closes the door to all forms of strategies. However, lotocracy doesn’t achieve majority. If every elector votes for A except one who votes for B, then B has a nonzero chance of winning. Alternatives that most electors are strongly against will be called extreme here.

Our electoral systems are terrible

The simplest voting system imaginable is the one-round uninominal voting system. It is the one used in the United States (Electoral College aside). This system trivially achieves majority. Yet, it fails at independence of irrelevant alternatives. In 2016, as he was running as an independent candidate, Bernie Sanders had to give up on his campaign, for all his presence would have done was taking votes away from Hillary Clinton. It is not unlikely that, had he not taken this decision, Donald Trump would have won the popular vote as well as the electoral one. Not only that, Bernie Sanders was incentivized to express unconditional support for Hillary Clinton’s platform, even ideas he strongly disagreed with. The slightest negative thing he would have said could have profited the Republican platform in some way. Not only had a candidate have to step back from the race, he also had to lie, making it two different ways he didn’t defend his ideas. This system also fails at strategyproofness in that small parties have very little chance of winning the election. As a result, supporters of small parties have to consider voting from “the least of two evils” and thus lie on their ballot. This has the effect of feeding a party duopoly that removes all incentive on the parties to serve the people, since the barrier to entry of competing smaller parties is humongous.

In France, the electoral system used is also uninominal, but it has two rounds so that the leading two parties of the first round face off in the second one. If a party has the majority in the first round, then it immediately wins, so this system achieves majority. However, it fails at independence of irrelevant alternatives for the same reason as before. Although there is no party duopoly, strategyproofness is still an issue because if a party you hate is likely to pass the first round, then it may be worth it not to vote for your party in favor of another one that would be more likely to win in the second round. This becomes more evident as the competition between second and third parties is tight. In 2012, unofficial statistics proved that centrist candidate François Bayrou would have beaten François Hollande in a face-off, yet he did not pass the first round. This system elected a candidate that the people would gladly replace by someone else if they had the opportunity. If fact, this article by Rémi Peyre explains that François Bayrou would have beaten any other candidate. Thus, this system fails at the Condorcet criterion.

The Condorcet criterion is actually very important. Imagine that the day after the election, a referendum is held to replace the winning candidate with François Bayrou. No matter who that candidate may have been, the people would approve the proposition. There is evidently some kind of unstability that the electoral system did not take into account, and this led a blatantly sub-optimal candidate into office. In fact, the winner of the election, François Hollande, finished his term with an approval rate of 17% and is, as of 2020, the only French president who didn’t bother running for reelection since at least 1958.

What about these alternatives?

Several alternatives are frequently proposed to the usual electoral systems. This section will explain their typical flaws.

Instant-runoff voting

This is an ordinal system where the electors’ first choices are used in a uninominal fashion. If a candidate has the majority, they win. Otherwise, the last candidate is eliminated, and another uninominal election is simulated with the remaining candidates until someone has the majority. Although this system is quite robust, it still provably fails at strategyproofness. Tactical voting would consist of choosing the order of eliminations wisely.

Score voting

The idea of this system is that you give each candidate a score, and the candidate with the best total score wins. In reality, the optimal strategy in this system is to give your favorite candidate the maximum score and zero to everyone else. We fall back to a uninominal electoral system.

Borda count

In this ordinal system, electors give each candidate n − k − 1 points where k refers to the rank of that candidate on the ballot. In this way, the top-ranked candidate gets n − 1 points while the last one gets zero. A variant starts at 1 instead of 0. The problem is that if your favorite candidate A is likely to lose against a candidate B, it may be viable to insincerely put B at the bottom of your ballot. The Borda count thus fails at strategyproofness.

Majority judgement

This one is actually not too bad. It consists of giving appreciations to candidates (e.g. very good, good, mixed, bad, very bad). If a candidate has the best median appreciation, they win. Otherwise, there are several ways to break ties.

The two impossibility theorems

As it turns out, two theorems seriously get in the way of having ideal electoral systems.

  • Arrow’s impossibility theorem: No ordinal electoral system can achieve both unanimity and independence of irrelevant alternatives without being a dictatorship.
  • Gibbard’s theorem: No electoral system can achieve strategyproofness for more than two alternatives without being a dictatorship.
Although Arrow’s theorem can be worked around by using non-ordinal electoral systems, Gibbard’s theorem applies to all electoral systems. It is therefore pointless to look for perfect electoral systems. Instead, the hunt should go toward systems that are too unpredictable to willingly manipulate in a particular direction.

The Randomized Condorcet Voting System

This last section details the electoral system that convinced me the most. It’s biggest strengths are that it is provably strategyproof in some cases and that it is made difficult to predict in other cases due to randomness.

The Condorcet method

The Condorcet method was an unsuccessful attempt by Nicolas de Condorcet at making the most natural electoral system that would achieve the Condorcet criterion. Although it initially failed, recent advances in game theory allowed to generalize it in a way that makes it a lot more useful.

Ballots

There are several ways ballots can be formed, but the idea is to organize duels between alternatives. In the most general form, the ballot is a grid where rows and columns are alternatives and the elector has to specify who is better on each square. Of course, this first approach is very wrong, but we can improve upon it. We call the complexity of a ballot the maximum amount of information the elector has to specify to fill it as a function of the number of alternatives n. It is not equal to the total number of valid ballots. This first grid ballot has a complexity of n2.

The first improvement comes from the fact that one cannot prefer an alternative over itself. This property is known as irreflexivity. This excludes the diagonal in the grid. The complexity falls down to n (n − 1).

The second improvement comes from the fact that from two alternatives A and B, one cannot simultaneously prefer A over B and B over A. This property is known as antisymmetry. This cuts the grid in half. The complexity falls down to n (n − 1) ÷ 2.

The last improvement comes from the fact that if, from three alternatives A, B and C, a same elector prefers A over B and B over C, then they prefer A over C. This property is known as transitivity and is one of the Von Neumann−Morgenstern axioms for rational behavior. Intuitively it may be possible to still prefer C over A, but the cyclical preferences that this entails are irrational and may be exploited for scams. A grid is no longer suited for this type of ballot. Instead, we will use ranges of ranks. The elector gives each alternative a range [a, b] of ranks. If the ranges of two alternatives overlap, they are considered equal, otherwise the one with greater ranges is considered better. This makes the complexity drop down to 2 n which scales a lot better than the previous ones. It is also still intuitive and leaves room for leaving alternatives unranked.

Condorcet winners

The goal of the Condorcet method is to find a Condorcet winner, that is an alternative that either ties or wins against all other ones. Conceptually, a directed graph called a duel graph represents all duels in the following way:

  • the vertices correspond to alternatives;
  • between two alternatives A and B, if more electors prefer A over B than the opposite, then the duel graph contains an arrow from A to B;
  • alternatives that tie have no arrow between them.
On such a graph, a Condorcet winner is given by a vertex that has no incoming arrow.

Unfortunately, the Condorcet winner is not always unique, nor does it always exist. Imagine an election between three alternatives A, B and C where a third of electors prefer A over B over C, a third prefer B over C over A, and a third prefer C over A over B. The resulting duel graph is cyclical although the individual preferences weren’t. It also has no Condorcet winner. This particular situation is commonly called the Condorcet paradox and was the major reason why the Condorcet method originally failed: even in the absence of ties between two alternatives, the Condorcet method as originally designed can fail at electing a winner.

The gold nugget of randomization

Fortunately, mathematics made huge progress since Condorcet’s days. The cyclical graph in the Condorcet paradox should remind you of something: it is equivalent to the graph explaining the rules of rock paper scissors. Now think outside the box: how do you win at rock paper scissors? Sure, there are strategies based on human player biases, but ultimately, no strategy can do better than a player who plays completely randomly. We will apply similar principles to decide an optimal strategy on a generalized rock paper scissors game based on our duel graph and deduce a probability distribution from which to choose the winner.

Formally, by generalized rock paper scissors game, we mean any 2-player, zero-sum, simultaneous game whose outcomes are either a tie or a win−lose with the same utility payoffs in all win−lose outcomes. A zero-sum game means that the total utility gained by the winners is always equal to the total utility lost by the losers. The other terms are either defined above or self-explanatory. Then, we define mixed strategies as strategies that choose a decision randomly, not necessarily according to a uniform probability distribution. Through mathematical wizardry that is beyond the scope of this text, it is possible to show that maximizing one’s expected payoff is equivalent to solving a certain linear program which can be done with the simplex algorithm or any other linear solver.

All of this work results in the Randomized Condorcet Voting System (RCVS). It may be difficult to understand all the mathematics behind it, but the next section will detail why its use is more than worth it.

So how good is it?

Now to examine the theoretical properties of the RCVS.

  • Condorcet criterion: Being a generalization of the Condorcet method, the RCVS trivially achieves the Condorcet criterion. Then, it also achieves majority and unanimity.
  • Independence of irrelevant alternatives: Since the ballots are not total orders, the RCVS is not subject to Arrow’s impossibility theorem. In 1997, Jean-François Laslier proved that if an alternative is outside the domain of the election’s optimal strategy, then removing it will not change the optimal strategy.
  • Strategyproofness: Gibbard’s theorem states that this property is unreachable in general cases, but it doesn’t rule out getting close to it. All Condorcet methods are strategyproof in the special case where more than half the electors have the same favorite alternative. The RCVS also achieves strategyproofness in the slightly more general case where a unique Condorcet winner exists (the difference is subtle, but you can picture some electors each preferring a different irrelevant candidate over A and agregate it in a way that A is still a Condorcet winner). Lê Nguyên Hoang proved that this property is very rare in the sense that any electoral system that achieves this property will behave the same as the RCVS in many cases.
  • Extreme-proofness: The RCVS will never elect an extreme alternative, meaning an alternative that a majority of electors are strongly against. This is a very good property whose lack was the best argument against lotocracy.

What about the Electoral College?

Although the RCVS isn’t incompatible with an Electoral College, as far as I know, there is currently no theoretical inquiry on what properties could be achieved with it. Personally, I did implement features in my code that could be used to simulate an Electoral College, but I would advise against its use until that work is done.
